potty provision in the urban

The Parisian “Vespasienne” and the “Café Achteck” from Berlin, both found in this world, are public urinals designed to be used in a standing position. They were manufactured in series and installed in the 19th century in their respective cities. In a time of the separate spheres ideology, they allowed abled body men to spend more time in public, but left especially women on a so-called “bladder leash” closely around their home (Lowe 2018, 29,57; Ramster, Greed, and Bichard 2018, 98). At the turn of the century Paris represented the pinnacle of urinal provision with 4000 Vespasiennes (Furrer 2010, 68; Richter 2020, 49).

Some of these urinals from the turn of the century still exist today and in many cases uphold a higher provision for people able to pee in a standing position. Additionally some cities even constructed new urinals that are only possible to use in a standing position (Greed 2003, 105; Ramster, Greed, and Bichard 2018, 96; Schubert 2020; Grönwall and Winker 2010). Since the 19th century construction has been primarily aimed at drunk men (Ramster, Greed, and Bichard 2018, 97). These developments can also be observed in many other European cities (Ramster, Greed, and Bichard 2018, 97; Furrer 2010, 68; Richter 2020, 49). Architectural historian and toilet activist Clara Greed even reports contrary developments, where “many traditional day-time city center toilets which met the day-time and evening needs of the general public, providing facilities for women and men, and for the disabled too, are being closed.”(Greed 2003, 105). To her this means an even bigger privilege for abled men through the shifting of resources "to meet the night-time needs of drinkers” (Greed 2003, 105).

In 2019 the dutch 19th century urinals lovingly called “plaskrul” (“pisscurl”) caused nation-wide protests after a woman was prosecuted in Amsterdam for peeing right next to one (Boffey 2017; Richter 2020, 49). “Amsterdam has 35 public urinals for men and only three public toilets for women. It was past the closing time of local bars, and Piening [the women prosecuted] was a couple of kilometers from the nearest women’s toilet facilities." The Guardian reported before the protests of the sparse provision in Amsterdam for people unable to urinate in a standing position began.

When I was sketching inside a Café Achteck in Berlin recently, a woman entered the octagon. I left to give her some privacy peeing. When she came out and I went back in to continue sketching the architecture, you could see the impracticality of the urinal for people peeing squatting. Her pee puddle was in the middle of the octagonal space, not at the sides as intended. The splashback was visible and seemingly her feet must have gotten a good amount of urine on them, because I could trace her wet foot steps out of the urinal. The judge who sparked the protest in Amsterdam claimed about urinating with a vulva in a standing urinal: "It would not be pleasant but it can be done." (Boffey 2017).
