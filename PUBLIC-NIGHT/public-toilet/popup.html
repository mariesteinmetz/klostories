representative public toilets

Cities and their municipal administrators have been shifting from focusing on public infrastructure for the needs and wellbeing of their citizens to becoming business-like entities striving for economic growth. This results in a global contest between cities regarding their attractiveness. This development has been going on since the 1980s and has us questioning who has a right to the city (Eick 1998; Heeg and Rosol 2007). In the wake of this attractiveness contest, public toilets can become a marketing tool for cities.

Increasingly, city centers and neighboring spaces are supposed to be cities’ visiting cards (Heeg and Rosol 2007). The motivation for municipalities to build and renovate public toilets in these areas is often to attract and retain tourists and other economically beneficial visitors there (Lowe 2018). In her comprehensive book “No Place To Go: How Public Toilets Fail Our Private Needs” (2018), Canadian journalist Lezlie Lowe describes how communities in British Columbia, the city of Denver and the small town of Lunenburg in Nova Scotia all prioritized public toilets to promote tourism in her comprehensive book. Even though these toilets seem to be aimed at visitors, Lowe highlights that local residents also benefit from the public toilets installation, especially if adaptations are made, like extensions to the opening times into the off season, as for example happened in Lunenburg. However, one has to consider that their design, their location, their opening times regarding time of day and season, remain dependent on changing governments and businesses interests. (Lowe 2018; Heeg and Rosol 2007)

Apart from the goal of attracting and retaining visitors and businesses, another factor motivating municipalities and businesses to build public toilets is evident in marketing campaigns accompanying some construction and renovation projects. This factor seems to aim either at promoting diversity or the purported welfare state goals of the current government or around promoting a certain image regarding the culture of the city, region or the culture of the respective country (Lowe 2018; The Tokyo Toilet Project, n.d.). For this, the architecture of the toilets, artworks in the establishments, or artworks that are specifically produced to support these marketing campaigns are used to spread these projects on a national or even global scale. The toilets become sites of representation.

A recent example for representative public toilets is “The Tokyo Toilet” project. Seventeen public toilets have been reimagined in Shibuya, the downtown district of Tokyo, since 2020 (The Tokyo Toilet Project, n.d.). The Shibuya Municipal Government, the Nippon Foundation, a non-governmental organization that manages the proceeds from Japanese motorboat races "for philanthropic purposes", the Japanese construction companies Daiwo House and TOTO, and the Shibuya City Tourism Association worked together to implement and continuously maintain and manage the project.

The opinion piece by Jungo Kanayama, the Representative Director of the Shibuya City Tourism Association,, which is uploaded on the “The Tokyo Toilets” Website, reads like a positive turn on Susanne Heegs and Marit Rosols paragraph on “Territorial Control Strategies” in “neoliberal city-politics in the global context” (2007), starting with describing Shibuya as “a theme park” for visitors. For him, the toilets seem to fulfill the function of making visitors' trip experiences better in providing quality toilets while simultaneously representing “Shibuya Cities [our city’s] uniqueness and character”.

The representational factor also seems to have played a role in motivating the toilets for government agencies, with Shibuya district mayor Ken Hasebe stating on the project's website that he hoped the toilets, like Shibuya's fashion and music, would send a message on a national and global scale..

And it seems to work: The toilets were designed by famous architects, including four of the nine Pritzker Prize winners from Japan (Tadao Andō, Toyo Ito, Shigeru Ban, Fumihiko Maki). For their famous designers, as well as for their representative and unique architecture, the toilets were featured in several international (architecture) magazines and newspapers like The Spaces, designboom, The Guardian, The Telegraph and Dezeen (Tucker 2020; Barandy 2021; McCurry 2020; Ryall 2023; Ravenscroft 2020). Additionally, German film-maker Wim Wenders directed the film “Perfect Days” revolving around the toilets, depicting the life of Hirayama, a fictional member of the cleaning staff (Wenders 2023). The movie was nominated for the Academy Awards and the actor who played Hirayama won the Palme D'Or, so naturally the motion picture drew wide international attention (The Economist 2023).

Ultimately, one keeps wondering if “The Tokyo Toilet” project highlighted the general importance of quality public toilets locally and globally, or if it simply sought to bring attention to Shibuya as an architecturally and/or culturally interesting tourist attraction. Were the toilets constructed to represent Japan’s “hospitality culture”(The Tokyo Toilet Project, n.d.) and “higher standard of hygiene” (‘THE TOKYO TOILET’, n.d.) or to provide more and better provision of public toilets to Tokyo’s residents? When do these buildings become more monuments than toilets? In replacing existing structures, provision in number didn’t increase. However, according to the project's website they increased the provision of more accessible toilets. Besides accessibility, do locals actually benefit from the architectural diversity? What will the effects of the representative factor of these “little jewels” (Wim Wenders, 2024) on gentrification and displacement in their neighborhoods be?
